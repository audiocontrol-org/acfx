<LessonPart n={1} id="concept" kicker="Concept" title="What a state-variable filter is">

A **state-variable filter** (SVF) is a single second-order structure that
produces a **lowpass**, **bandpass**, and **highpass** output *at the same
time*, from the same internal state, on every sample. You do not pick one and
rebuild the filter to get another — all three fall out of one topology. Two
controls shape them: **cutoff frequency**, the corner where the filter turns
over, and **resonance** (Q), how much the response peaks right at that corner.

That combination — three responses for the price of one, two intuitive knobs,
a topology that stays stable and cheap — is why the SVF is the *hello-world* of
analog-style filters. It is the first filter worth building because it teaches
the two ideas every later filter reuses: a frequency-setting coefficient and a
resonance-setting coefficient feeding back on a pair of integrators.

Analog-prototype, the three outputs are three numerators over **one shared
denominator** — same poles, different zeros. That shared denominator is the
whole point: cutoff and resonance move the *poles*, and every output feels it
together.

<MathWell label="Transfer functions" note="one denominator · three numerators">
<pre>{`H_LP(s) = ω₀²          / (s² + (ω₀/Q)·s + ω₀²)
H_BP(s) = (ω₀/Q)·s     / (s² + (ω₀/Q)·s + ω₀²)
H_HP(s) = s²           / (s² + (ω₀/Q)·s + ω₀²)`}</pre>
</MathWell>

In the discrete world the SVF is a tiny feedback network of two integrators.
Signal flow: the input has the lowpass and a resonance-scaled bandpass
subtracted from it to form the **highpass**; the highpass is integrated to make
the **bandpass**; the bandpass is integrated again to make the **lowpass** — and
those two feed straight back into the next sample. One coefficient `f` sets the
integrator gain (cutoff), one coefficient `q` sets the damping (resonance).

<MathWell label="Discrete recurrence" note="Chamberlin form · per sample">
<pre>{`HP[n] = x[n] − LP[n−1] − q·BP[n−1]
BP[n] = f·HP[n] + BP[n−1]
LP[n] = f·BP[n] + LP[n−1]

f = 2·sin(π·fc/fs)          q ← resonance`}</pre>
</MathWell>

Lower `q` narrows the peak (higher Q); as `q` approaches zero the corner rings
harder until the filter self-oscillates. Keep that picture in mind — you are
about to *hear* it, then *see* the pole that causes it march toward the unit
circle.

</LessonPart>

<LessonPart n={3} id="observe-it" kicker="Observe it" title="See the filter respond">

Here is the same filter as a **bench analyzer**, painted live from the real
compiled analysis code — not redrawn in JavaScript. Three screens: the
**frequency response** (what each frequency is multiplied by), the **z-plane
poles and zeros** (where the filter's resonance lives), and the **impulse
response** (how it rings in time).

Sweep cutoff and watch the whole response slide along the frequency axis. Then
raise resonance and watch the **pole climb toward the unit circle** — the exact
moment the response grows its peak and the impulse rings longer. This is the
transfer function from Part 1 made visible: the pole radius *is* the resonance.
